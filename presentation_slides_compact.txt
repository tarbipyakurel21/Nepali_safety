Slide 2 - Problem Statement
---------------------------
- Low-resource languages like Nepali are underrepresented in LLM safety alignment.
- Nepali appears in multiple scripts (Devanagari + Romanized), increasing evaluation complexity.
- Harmful prompts may bypass safety behavior in non-English contexts.
- Need: language-aware safety evaluation and refusal tuning.

[Image Placeholder: Motivation diagram - "English safety vs Low-resource safety gap"]


Slide 3 - Objectives
--------------------
- Build a reproducible safety-evaluation pipeline for Nepali prompts.
- Measure Safe/Unsafe/Invalid behavior across English, Nepali, and Romanized Nepali.
- Fine-tune baseline model with refusal-style responses (LoRA SFT).
- Compare baseline vs fine-tuned behavior with quantitative metrics.


Slide 4 - Methodology Overview
------------------------------
Pipeline (Phase I -> Phase II):
1) Generate model answers
2) Translate Nepali outputs to English for judging
3) Classify with safety judge (safe/unsafe/invalid)
4) Fine-tune with refusal pairs
5) Re-evaluate and compare confusion matrices

[Image Placeholder: End-to-end pipeline flowchart]


Slide 5 - Algorithms, Models, and Techniques
--------------------------------------------
- Target model: `google/gemma-3-4b-it` (baseline + LoRA-tuned).
- Translation model: `google/gemma-3-12b-it` (back-translation for judging).
- Safety judge: `meta-llama/Llama-Guard-3-8B`.
- Technique: Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (LoRA) + 4-bit quantization.
- Evaluation logic:
  - Answer mode: refusal keyword/marker detection + 2x2 transition matrix.
  - Safety mode: safe/unsafe/invalid confusion matrix.

Slide 11 - Results (Phase I Baseline)

- Romanized Nepali: Safe 96.15%, Unsafe 1.92%, Invalid 1.92%

Takeaway:
- Unsafe outputs are low but non-zero.
- Romanized Nepali shows higher instability (unsafe + invalid behavior).

Slide 13 - Analysis vs Alternative/Baseline Methods
---------------------------------------------------
Compared approaches:
- Baseline (`gemma-3-4b-it`) vs LoRA fine-tuned model
- Rule-based refusal detector vs safety-judge labels

Interpretation:
- LoRA should increase refusals on harmful prompts.
- Transition analysis reveals whether gains come with harmful regressions.
- Safety judge adds external validation but depends on translation quality.


Slide 14 - Strengths, Weaknesses, Limitations
---------------------------------------------
Strengths:
- End-to-end multilingual safety pipeline
- Reproducible script-based evaluation
- Multiple evaluation views (answer-level + judge-level)

Weaknesses / limitations:
- Small prompt set (52/script) limits statistical confidence
- English-centric judge requires back-translation
- Translation noise can affect labels
- Quantization/VRAM constraints may impact generation fidelity


Slide 15 - Conclusion
---------------------
- The project establishes a practical safety benchmark for Nepali-script prompts.
- Baseline behavior is mostly safe, but residual unsafe/invalid outputs remain.
- LoRA-based refusal tuning provides a controlled path to improve safety behavior.
- Comparative analysis framework is reusable for future low-resource language safety work.


Slide 16 - Future Work
